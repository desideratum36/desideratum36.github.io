Introduction/Background

Polymers are widely used in various applications. One of the most important properties considered in the engineering application of a polymer is its glass transition temperature; since it determines the dependence of its mechanical properties on temperature and affects everything about the polymer, from its flexibility and durability to its optimal operating temperature range. [1]

Polymer design spaces are massive, and manual experimentation has never been an efficient way to determine the properties of potential polymers. Recent machine learning approaches have seen great success in polymer property prediction. [2], [3], [4] We believe our project will replicate the success of these studies, contributing to efficient polymer materials discovery by predicting glass transition temperatures and property classes of potential polymers given their structure.

Background Concepts

  • Polymers: A polymer is composed of large molecules formed of chains consisting of many small molecules (monomers); some examples include polyethylene terephthalate (PET), Nylon-6,6, and polyvinyl chloride (PVC). Most polymers are carbon-based, with oxygen and hydrogen being common constituents; other elements, such as nitrogen, sulfur, fluorine, etc., may also be present. [1]
  • SMILES Strings: Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System (SMILES) strings are an ASCII description of chemical structure; a depth-first traversal of a molecule’s chemical graph–nodes -> atoms, edges -> bonds. Packages such as RDKit can extract molecular/elemental descriptors from a polymer’s SMILES strings. [2]
  • Glass Transition Temperature: The glass transition temperature is the temperature at which an amorphous polymer transitions from a ductile, workable state to a rigid, glassy one. Polymers below their glass transition temperatures are strong and hard, while polymers above their glass transition temperatures are ductile and flexible. [1]
  • Polymer Classes: Polymers are divided into classes based on their backbone structure. Polyesters have a backbone ester (-COO-) linkage, leading to high polarizability, strength, and chemical resistance; polyethers/polyoxides/polyacetals have a backbone -O—linkage, resulting in flexible chains with good solubility and low melting temperatures. [1]

Dataset

Our project utilizes the “Extra dataset with SMILES,Tg,PID,Polimers Class” dataset, hosted on Kaggle and originally sourced from PoLyInfo. It contains SMILES strings, glass transition temperatures, and polymer class labels for 7,174 distinct monomer compositions. [5], [6]

Problem Definition

Problem: Build a supervised machine learning model to predict the glass transition temperature and the polymer class of a polymer from its SMILES string.

Motivation: Experimental discovery of glass transition temperatures is time-consuming. Accurate prediction will accelerate polymer design, enabling rapid discovery of polymers while reducing resources spent on physical experimentation. This approach promotes efficiency and sustainability in materials science by minimizing the need for lengthy and costly lab work. [2], [3]

Methods

Data Preprocessing

Our preprocessing pipeline is designed to convert raw SMILES strings into a feature set suitable for machine learning models:

  • SMILES to Descriptors: We will use the RDKit package to determine molecular and elemental descriptors for each molecule from its SMILES string. Examples include molecular weight, aromaticity, atomic adjacencies, and atom-pair counts. [2]
  • Feature Selection: To select informative features from a vast set of potential descriptors, we will use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) via scikit-learn to reduce dimensionality and find features that best summarize the dataset.
  • Encoding and Normalization: For classification, polymer classes will be one-hot encoded. All selected features and the glass transition temperatures will be normalized to ensure consistent scaling across the dataset.

ML Models

We will implement and evaluate three distinct models for regression and classification tasks:

  • Random Forest Regressor (scikit-learn): An ensemble model effective at handling nonlinear relationships and identifying relevant features for predicting glass transition temperature. [2], [3], [4]
  • Gradient Boosting Classifier (scikit-learn): An effective classifier for polymer class prediction, as it performs well with sparse input features like adjacency matrices.
  • Neural Network (PyTorch): A versatile model for both regression and classification. We anticipate that a neural network will capture deep, complex connections within our large dataset, leading to high accuracy with minimal overfitting. [3], [4]

Potential Results and Discussion

Quantitative Metrics

Regression Metrics

Mean Squared Error (MSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and R² for glass transition temperature prediction.

Classification Metrics

F-1 Score and the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC) for polymer classification.

Project Goals

  • Achieve a validation set Mean Absolute Error (MAE) less than 5 K for glass transition temperature prediction.
  • Achieve 95% polymer class classification accuracy on the validation data set.
  • Reduce energy and material used in physical testing by creating a model for virtual screening, increasing the sustainability of materials discovery.

Expected Results

We expect high accuracy in classification since identifying a polymer's backbone structure should be a relatively simple task for the models. We anticipate that ensemble models like Random Forest and Gradient Boosting will perform well on this dataset. By rigorously selecting features through PCA, we aim to reduce overfitting and improve the generalization performance of our models.
